The wolf licked his lips as he left the pile of broken sticks behind and followed the two trembling pigs down the lane. Ahead of him stood a solid brick house, red and square, with smoke curling calmly from its chimney. The last pig was inside, placing another brick near the hearth when he heard the frantic knocking. “Brother! Let us in!” He opened the door at once and pulled them inside. One glance at their shaking knees told him everything. The wolf’s shadow fell across the doorway. He knocked slowly. Once. Twice. “Little pigs,” he growled, “let me come in.” The last pig stepped forward, steady and tall. “Not by the hair of our chinny chin chin.” The wolf’s eyes flashed. “Then I’ll huff, and I’ll puff, and I’ll blow your house in!” The last pig did not move. The wolf filled his lungs and blew with all his might. The windows rattled. The fire flickered. But the walls did not budge. Again the wolf huffed. Harder. Longer. His face turned red. His breath came out in angry bursts. Still, the bricks held firm. Inside, the last pig spoke calmly to his brothers. “Stay back. The walls are thick. I laid every brick tight with mortar. He cannot blow them down.” Outside, the wolf snarled in frustration. He pushed against the door. He clawed at the sides. He circled the house, searching for a weakness. Then he looked up. “The chimney,” he muttered. The last pig heard the scrape of claws on brick and knew at once what the wolf was planning. Quickly, he hung a large iron pot over the fire and filled it with water. Flames crackled higher beneath it. The wolf squeezed into the chimney and began to slide down, grinning to himself. Down… down… down— SPLASH! He landed straight into the steaming pot. With a howl so loud it echoed across the valley, the wolf shot back up the chimney, covered in soot and scalded pride. He leapt from the roof and ran without looking back. The last pig closed the fireplace door and brushed the soot from his hooves. His brothers stared at him in awe. “It wasn’t magic,” he said quietly. “It was preparation.” And in the strong brick house, built with care and patience, the three pigs were finally safe.
